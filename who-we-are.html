Our Story

History & Accomplishments

In 1987, 220 pristine acres of wildland habitat were threatened by residential development on Volcan Mountain. The Volcan Mountain Foundation was born in 1988 out of the quiet diplomacy of concerned Julian citizens seeking to retain the land in its natural state.

1988

Foundation Established

VMF formed to protect 220 acres of wildland threatened by development on Volcan Mountain — born from community action in Julian, CA.

2000s

Wilderness Preserve Created

Through conservancy partnerships, VMF facilitated San Diego County's Volcan Mountain Wilderness Preserve, adding over 17,000 acres and bringing conserved land to ~31,000 acres.

2009–11

Nature Center Launched

VMF acquired nearly 400 centrally-located acres to launch environmental education and habitat management — seeding the Volcan Mountain Nature Center.

2020

East Side Acquisition

23 acres acquired on the east side of the Volcan Mountains, straddling the San Luis Rey Watershed and the Salton Sea Basin divide.

2022

Copper Spur Ranch

42-acre Copper Spur Ranch acquired, preserving oak woodland and riparian land at the San Dieguito Watershed headwaters.

Today

31,000+ Acres Protected

VMF continues working toward preservation of the entire 15-mile Volcan Mountain range through science, partnership, and community engagement.

Mission, Vision & Goals

Why We Preserve Volcan Mountain

"To preserve Volcan Mountain in its natural state for all generations."

Rising over 5,000 feet, Volcan Mountain is visible from the Pacific coastline 60 miles away. It is one of the last privately-owned and relatively untouched areas of Southern California — an extraordinary natural treasure.

Why it matters:

  • 💧 Abundant water & watershed protection
  • 🦎 Unique habitat for sensitive, rare & endangered species
  • 🔗 Critical wildlife corridors & open space connectivity
  • 🏛️ Rich cultural history of San Diego's backcountry
  • 🌄 Recreation for all San Diego County residents

How we preserve it:

  • 🏞️ Acquiring land from willing sellers for permanent protection
  • 📖 Outdoor Education & Public Outreach programs
  • 🌱 Reintroduction of long-lost native species
  • 🔥 Restoring burned & disturbed habitat
  • 🔬 Research & monitoring of post-fire reestablishment

About Volcan Mountain

Natural History

The Volcan Mountain range rises over 5,000 feet above the Julian backcountry, its profile visible from the Pacific Coast 60 miles to the west. The range encompasses a remarkable variety of habitats — from dense chaparral and coastal sage scrub at lower elevations to ancient oak woodland, pine forests, and wet meadows near the summit.

  • Oak Woodland — Ancient blue oaks, canyon live oaks, and valley oaks define the landscape
  • Watersheds — Headwaters of the Santa Ysabel Creek and San Dieguito River originate here
  • Wildlife — Mountain lions, mule deer, golden eagles, and hundreds of bird species
  • Rare Plants — Multiple sensitive and rare plant species found nowhere else in San Diego County
  • Cultural History — Kumeyaay people used the mountain for generations as a seasonal resource area

Geology

The oldest rocks identified in San Diego County date from the middle of the Mesozoic era—age of the dinosaurs—190 to 150 million years ago. The land in the southwest formed an island arc, similar to Japan. To the west was a deep sea trench and to the east a shallow sea. This geological pattern forms what is called a "subduction zone." The Pacific plate to the west slid into the North American plate to the East. This action created long periods of great volcanic activity, followed by erosion processes.

The flat plain that had been the west coastal area, tipped upward like a giant wedge, forming the Peninsular Ranges from south of Los Angeles to the center of Baja California, of which Volcan Mountain is a part. Jurassic/Cretaceous rocks of the batholithic period are composed of undifferentiated granite and tonalities. Also found is Triassic metamorphic rock composed of micaceous schist, gneiss and quartzite, known as Julian schist—layered with rich ore-bearing quartz containing gold of unusually high grade.

The Elsinore Fault, a sub-parallel fracture of the San Andreas Fault, traverses Volcan Mountain in a northwest/southeast direction. It has produced many small earthquakes over the past years. We truly feel the earth move under our feet in the Volcan region!

Water Sheds

The Volcan Mountain Range is a main contributor to quenching San Diego’s thirst! It feeds three of the largest watersheds in the San Diego Basin (flowing west directly into the Pacific Ocean). San Felipe Creek starts high atop Volcan Mountain and drops sharply east to the desert, feeding the Anza-Borrego Watershed and Colorado Basin.

An average of 30+ inches of precipitation a year contribute to pristine source waters like Ironside and Catfish Springs that feed the San Luis Rey, San Dieguito, and San Diego River watersheds. Hahachepang means "Where the waters come from" and refers to several springs. It's the traditional Native American name for the Volcan Mountain Range. Protecting headwater areas is the single most effective way of maintaining downstream water quality, making conservation efforts on Volcan crucially important.

Plants & Animals

Volcan Mountain is an extremely pristine and biologically diverse area. Over 30 different types of plant communities have been identified which provide habitat for a wide variety of wildlife species. The relatively undisturbed nature of the mountain provides a stronghold for some of San Diego’s rare plant and animal species. Preliminary biological studies have recorded 45 species of mammals, over 150 birds, 36 reptiles, 12 amphibians, and an unknown number of insects and other invertebrates.

Human History & How Volcan Got Its Name

The people who lived on Volcan somewhere in the vicinity of 2,000-200 years ago, called themselves The People, or Iipay. The Iipay lived in small bands in the canyons and on the rims, relying on abundant food sources like acorns, berries, roots, grass seeds, deer and rabbit. As early as 1775, the Native People began to see their way of life change dramatically as Spanish and Mexican colonizers arrived. A branch of the California Missions, the Asistencia de Santa Ysabel, was built in 1818 at the foot of Volcan’s western slope.

Following the Spanish conquests, Anglo-Americans, trappers, miners, loggers and ranchers poured into the region. Of the 15,500 acres of the resulting Santa Ysabel Reservation land, 8,000 acres lie on the northwestern flank of Volcan Mountain. Today approximately 75 landowners have holdings on Volcan Mountain, including the Iipay Nation of Santa Ysabel.

How Did Volcan Get Its Name?
Some have said that Volcan is a version of the word volcano and that early settlers thought they saw volcanic rock and ash. The range is actually covered with granite, not volcanic rock! A 1972 article suggests another origin: In 1844 a man named Edward Stokes was a son in-law of the Mexican land grant owner of Rancho Santa Ysabel. The name of the ranch at the time was Rancho de Santa Ysabel Balcón. Looking from west to east, the curvature of the mountain looks like a balcony (balcón in Spanish). Others note that on a map around 1860, there was a family said to be living on the mountain by the name of Vaulcan.

About Volcan Mountain

Geology

The oldest rocks identified in San Diego County date from the middle of the Mesozoic era — age of the dinosaurs — 190 to 150 million years ago. The land in the southwest formed an island arc, similar to Japan, with a deep sea trench to the west and a shallow sea to the east. This geological pattern forms a "subduction zone", where the Pacific plate slid beneath the North American plate, creating long periods of great volcanic activity followed by erosion.

The Southern California 'Batholith' rose out of the molten mantle, forming the Peninsular Ranges from south of Los Angeles to the center of Baja California — of which Volcan Mountain is a part. Jurassic/Cretaceous rocks of the batholithic period are composed of undifferentiated granite and tonalites. Julian schist, exposed through erosion, was found to be layered with rich ore-bearing quartz containing gold of unusually high grade (940–980 fine).

The Elsinore Fault, a sub-parallel fracture of the San Andreas Fault, traverses Volcan Mountain in a northwest/southeast direction. The Volcan area is capable of a 7.3 earthquake — most recently, the 2010 Easter Sunday 7.2 earthquake from El Centro rocked the Julian area.

Watersheds & Source Waters

Water

The Volcan Mountain Range is a main contributor to quenching San Diego's thirst. It is a vital regional resource — in San Diego County, water from Volcan Mountain feeds three of the largest watersheds in the San Diego Basin. San Felipe Creek starts high atop Volcan Mountain and drops sharply east to the desert, feeding the Anza-Borrego Watershed and Colorado Basin that flows to the Salton Sea and the Gulf of California.

An average of 30+ inches of precipitation a year contribute to pristine source waters such as Ironside and Catfish Springs that feed the San Luis Rey, San Dieguito, and San Diego River watersheds, flowing 55–60 miles to San Diego's metropolitan communities and coastline — comprising 1,366 square miles of San Diego County's coastal watersheds.

"Hahachepang" means 'Where the waters come from' — the traditional Native American name for the Volcan Mountain Range. For thousands of years, human and animal communities have known that Volcan Mountain is a source of life.

Protecting headwater areas is the single most effective way of maintaining downstream water quality. Conservation efforts like fuel reduction and management ensure that wildfire severity is kept in check, minimizing erosion and soil contamination in water bodies to protect habitat and water resources downstream.

Cultural Heritage

Human History

The people who lived on Volcan somewhere in the vicinity of 2,000–200 years ago called themselves the Iipay — "The People" in their language. The Iipay lived in small bands in the canyons and on the rims, producing little in the way of what we today would call 'trash.' Archeologists have found artifacts on the eastern side of the mountain dating back 6,000 years.

As early as 1775, some Native People began to see their way of life change dramatically as Spanish and Mexican colonizers arrived. A branch of the California Missions, the Asistencia de Santa Ysabel, was built in 1818 at the foot of Volcan's western slope — the focal point of 'Indian affairs' once the Iipay were moved and forced to adopt Christianity and an agricultural lifestyle.

The ensuing conflicts brought in the U.S. Federal Government, which designated the surrounding 15,500 acres as the Santa Ysabel Reservation in 1875. Of those 15,500 acres, 8,000 lie on the northwestern flank of Volcan Mountain. Today approximately 75 landowners have holdings on Volcan Mountain, including the Iipay Nation of Santa Ysabel.

The earliest known cabins were built and occupied on the mountain as far back as 1863. The largest ranch was the 6,000-acre Grand family ranch on the east side in Arkansas Canyon — supporting vineyards, crops, cattle, sheep, and a 2-story stone home.

Etymology

How Did Volcan Mountain Get Its Name?

There are differing sources and possible explanations as to how Volcan Mountain got its name. Some have said that Volcan is a version of the word volcano and that early settlers thought they saw volcanic rock and ash — though the range is covered with granite, not volcanic rock.

A Times-Advocate article from 1972 traces the name to around 1844: Edward Stokes, son-in-law of Mexican land grant owner Jose Joaquin Ortega, managed the land grant known as Rancho de Santa Ysabel Balcón. It is said that if you look at the valley of Santa Ysabel from west to east, the curvature of the mountain looks like a balcony — balcón in Spanish.

On a map around 1860, a family named Vaulcan was said to be living on the mountain, though this family name did not appear on official records at that time. A family by the name of Vaulcan lives in Mesa Grande today.
