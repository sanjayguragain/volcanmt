Conservation & Stewardship

Preservation & Land Protection

Working with conservancy partners, VMF's vision is to acquire land for preservation from willing sellers and protect the entire 52,000-acre Volcan Mountain range.

🌲

Land Acquisition

We facilitate purchases of land from willing sellers to permanently protect habitat, water, and open space. Since 1988 we've conserved over 31,000 acres in the Volcan Mountain range.

🌿

Habitat Restoration

Managing invasive species, reintroducing native plants, reforesting burned habitat, and monitoring post-fire plant and animal recovery across the mountain range.

🏞️

Stewardship Planning

Developing and modeling sustainable land management practices — from non-native species control to watershed restoration and long-term ecological health.

Preservation Projects and Accomplishments

The foundation's Focused Planning Area (FPA) is on traditional Kumeyaay Land. Since 1989, through facilitation efforts of the Volcan Mountain Foundation (VMF) and its partners, more than 19,000 acres of land have been acquired from willing sellers, bringing conserved land within the range to approximately 42,000 acres.

After two decades of facilitating acquisitions, VMF became a land owner for the first time in 2009 with the acquisition of the former Hunters’ Camp property. VMF partnered with The Nature Conservancy and the County of San Diego to phase the acquisition which also resulted in adjacent lands being added to the Volcan Mountain Wilderness Preserve. With the addition of the Sky Island Trail to the ridgeline and the new Sky Island Trail Viewscope offering views from Anza Borrego to the Pacific Ocean, VMF has an outdoor laboratory supporting youth and adult education throughout the year.

In 2010, a 37-acre parcel adjacent to the former Hunters' Camp property was acquired by VMF, in partnership with conservation easement funds from the County of San Diego. In 2011, a 50-acre piece along an easterly-facing ridgeline was acquired through a generous gift. Closing escrow on the same day was another 37-acre acquisition at the headwaters to Santa Ysabel Creek. VMF's new Volcan Mountain Nature Center is the home to the Volcan Mountain Education Center honoring Keith and Priscilla Webb and Family, with full classroom facilities as well as the Watershed Trail and Native Plant Nursery.

In 2020, VMF acquired 23 acres of unspoiled wildlands on the east side of the Volcan Mountains overlooking the San Felipe Valley. The habitat and connectivity of this property makes it an important win in the broader vision to protect and preserve the Volcan Mountains.

Scientific Stewardship

Wildlife & Biodiversity Monitoring

Rigorous science guides our conservation decisions

🦅

Rare Bird Surveys

Annual raptor and songbird surveys track breeding populations and migration patterns across the mountain range, contributing to regional biodiversity databases.

🌺

Rare Plant Monitoring

Multiple sensitive plant species found only in San Diego County are monitored annually, with population data guiding habitat protection decisions.

🐾

Wildlife Corridors

Volcan Mountain serves as a critical linkage zone between the coast and inland desert. VMF monitors and protects wildlife movement corridors for mountain lion, mule deer, and other species.

Badger Research: Badgers Are Finally Back at Volcan Mountain!

Since 2011 scientists with the US Geological Survey (USGS), with grant funding from the SANDAG TransNet Environmental Mitigation Program (EMP), have been conducting Badger Conservation Research in watersheds connected to the Volcan Mountain range. Biologist Cheryl Brehme with the USGS Western Ecological Research Center (WERC) is leading the research and is partnering with Heath Smith, handler with the Conservation Canines program, and his badger-sniffing Australian cattle dog, Pips.

According to Brehme, "This badger research is important because it’s part of a broader effort to measure the success of regional initiatives to preserve and link wildlife habitat. Badger movements are a good indicator of the wildlife connectivity of upland habitats." Recently a significant badger population has been identified on part of the Volcan Mountain Wilderness Preserve.

Saving Oaks: Responding to Goldspotted Oak Borer (GSOB)

Julian to Descanso is at the epicenter of devastation from Goldspotted Oak Borer (GSOB). GSOB is an exotic, invasive insect in southern California that has caused elevated levels of tree mortality for several years. It represents the most significant threat to red oak species in southern California, and is considered a critical conservation threat.

In January 2018 an entomologist specializing in GSOB and a forest technician from the US Forest Service surveyed oaks on parts of the 398 acres owned and managed by VMF. They determined that GSOB is well-established on Volcan Mountain and is causing significant tree mortality to large-diameter oak trees. Combating GSOB and maintaining oaks on Volcan Mountain will be a long-term investment, requiring oak monitoring teams, outreach to raise awareness ("Buy It, Where You Burn It"), and reforestation efforts.

Plant Nerds on Volcan Mountain

August turned into a plant nerd month on Volcan Mountain. VMF had researchers from SDSU's Soils Ecology Restoration Group (SERG) exploring the mixed conifer forests and oak woodlands. They were especially looking forward to seeing Volcan's big cone Douglas firs—a rarity at the southern end of its range on Volcan. Another rarity is the San Felipe Monardella (part of the mint family), and native milkweed, which is an important food source for monarch butterflies.

With this past winter's abundant rains, the local mountains are experiencing a mast year, with conifers producing copious amounts of cones. In partnership with a geneticist at the US Forest Service, VMF is leading cone harvesting for big cone Douglas firs. The harvested cones will be propagated by the forestry service for future restoration efforts. Another new partnership with the San Diego chapter of the California Native Plant Society (CNPS-SD) has resulted in rare plant surveys on Volcan.

Education & Outreach

Inspiring the Next Generation

VMF's Education-Outreach program brings environmental stewardship into classrooms and takes students to the mountain to experience the importance of habitat protection, watershed health, and natural history firsthand.

  • Classroom Programs — Curriculum-linked environmental education for K–12 students throughout San Diego County
  • Field Experiences — Guided hikes and hands-on restoration projects on Volcan Mountain
  • Sky Island Trail — Ancient oak, pine & cedar grove with Pipe Scope panoramic views
  • Nature Center Programs — Discovery-based learning at the Volcan Mountain Nature Center
  • Education Advisory Committee — Committed educators shaping our curriculum and partnerships

Art & Culture

Art & Volcan Mountain

Artistic expression has always played an important role in the Volcan Mountain Foundation. Just after VMF was incorporated in 1988, world-acclaimed artist and Julian local, James Hubbell, painted a watercolor of the mountain that was made into a poster to raise funds for the infant organization.

In 1990, with local volunteers, Hubbell built a beautiful gateway of native rock and cedar beams at the trailhead. His son Brennan built the tall iron sculptures in front of the gateway; Bill Porter added organic metalwork; Mirko Mrakajic carved the creatures on the wooden beams.

Joe Garcia, another internationally acclaimed Julian artist, donates posters and paintings to VMF's annual fundraiser and was the first honoree when VMF inaugurated the 'Artist of the Year' concept in 2010. San Diego photographer Blair Shamel has been documenting the seasons on Volcan since 2009.

Full Art History Artist Residency

The Gateway to the Summit

Once the first 220 acres had been secured for public use in 1989, the Board asked James Hubbell to design an entrance to the trail on the west face of the mountain that would celebrate the passageway to the high summit and cause people to pause and appreciate that they were entering a special place. In 1990, with local volunteers spearheaded by Caryn Brause and Vicki Bergstrom, Mr. Hubbell built a beautiful gateway of native rock and cedar beams collected from and milled on nearby Palomar Mountain.

Using James’s design, his son Brennan built the tall iron sculptures in front of the gateway. Bill Porter added the organic metalwork on the back side of the entrance and Mirko Mrakajic carved the creatures on the wooden beams. A great story is when Mirko finished the carvings; a county official was not happy because he thought the carved figure of "Universal Man" looked too "demonic." Mirko, in his humble way, said nothing but quietly turned it into a frog!

James and stone mason, Vincent Guerrero, trained volunteers to build the walls and kiva (seating area) with native stone gathered from nearby streambeds and road cuts. An exquisite cedar bench was hand-carved by Ken Wright in memory of local biologist and outdoorsman, Clint Powell. The bench is located near the summit and serves as a reminder of all our wonderful artists and local volunteers.

Volcan Stories & Kids With Cameras

Photography, Film & Youth Visual Arts

VMF's Kids With Cameras program gives young photographers access to Volcan Mountain and teaches visual storytelling about conservation. Student photographs have been exhibited publicly and used in VMF outreach.

The Marjorie and Joseph Rubenson Endowment for Art and Science supports an annual artist residency on Volcan Mountain.
